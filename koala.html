KOALA

Koalas (Phascolarctos cinereus) are tree-dwelling marsupials native to Australia. Although they are often called "koala bears," they are not bears at all. Koalas belong to a group of mammals known as marsupials, which carry and nurse their young in pouches. They are most commonly found in eucalyptus forests along the eastern and southeastern coasts of Australia.

Koalas have thick, grey fur with a white chest, a large black nose, and fluffy ears. They have strong limbs and sharp claws, which help them climb and grip onto trees. Koalas sleep for up to 20 hours a day due to their low-energy diet, which consists almost entirely of eucalyptus leaves. These leaves are tough, toxic to most animals, and low in nutrition, but koalas have a special digestive system to process them.

Koalas are solitary and territorial animals. They communicate using a range of vocalizations, including loud bellows during mating season. Because of habitat loss and bushfires, koalas are considered a vulnerable species and are protected by law in many parts of Australia.
